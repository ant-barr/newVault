Course Description, Prerequisites & Course Modality

This economics course is taught at a business school for both business and nonbusiness majors. The course focuses on helping the beginning economics student develop a basic technical understanding of the subject matter. As an introductory course in economics, using the Arnold economics textbook, the class is designed to learn some concepts originally found in classic economic literature, combined with updated or refined theories from modern research. Being true to the great body of economic literature, the textbook mentions how many economists rank Keyness The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money alongside Adam Smiths An Inquiry into the Wealth of Nations and Karl Marxs Das Kapital as the most influential economic treatises ever written. Alongside these great bodies of work, the text includes new economic research such as Endogenous and New Growth Theories to be explored.

Nevertheless, the suggested supplemental reading (see course materials section below), while not exhaustive nor required reading for class, is given for those seeking further explanation (especially non-business majors) on why and how societies and its resources are organized. It provides context on the

subject matter; and hence, some degree of depth and breadth, setting the student on a path of intellectual discovery and awareness that allows him/her to further develop a more profound strategic worldview on how economics play in the geopolitical world stage, and as well as in their daily lives.

As per the college catalog, this course provides an introduction to the economy. Topics include national income and output, unemployment, inflation, market forces and economic growth, international linkages (such as trade deficits), and economic, social, and political structures and institutions such as fiscal and monetary policies and the Federal Reserve System). In short, this course will provide a fundamental understanding of economics as a science by providing an overview of modern principles and theories that explain different economic forces, variables, and outcomes in an economy.

MODE OF LEARNING

This course will be a traditional Face-to-Face. As such, the student is expected to show up physically to class, listen to a Power-Point lecture and take notes. Your learning will be augmented using assigned textbook chapter readings and online digital technology (e.g., YouTube videos, lecture note handouts, assignments, and select readings) using the UTRGV online Blackboard platform. Your instructors will provide you with feedback on assigned work, communicating with you electronically (via blackboard & or email), and if need to, be available to meet as defined on this syllabus.

Teaching Philosophy

Again, the class instruction will be provided, through face-to-face lectures, guided by power point presentations. Each student is responsible for their learning and studying, including textbook weekly chapter readings. The instructors role is to help students learn by leading the learning process through lectures. Each student must be prepared prior to class lectures by:

  1. the beginning of each week, check syllabus, emails, and blackboard for any instructor announcements.
  2. Read any assigned chapter material listed on Blackboards weekly course material folder, prior to class lectures.
  3. From Blackboard, print and review the Power Point lecture handout. Use it to follow along and take notes, while listening to lectures.

Learning Objectives/Outcomes for the course:

By the end of the semester students should be able to:

  1. Understand concepts of scarcity, opportunity cost, and production possibility frontier
  2. Understand the aggregate supply and demand curves, and equilibrium price.
  3. Understand change in demand vs changes in quantity demand and change in supply vs. changes in quantity supplied.
  4. Understand and calculate inflation, unemployment rate, and real GDP.
  5. Interpret the use of classical and Keynesian fiscal and monetary policy.
  6. Understand post-Keynesian monetary and fiscal policies.

COURSE DROPS

Please consider the following information when referencing course drops. Instructor-initiated drops can have significant financial consequences for students. According to UTRGV policy, students may drop any class without penalty earning a grade of DR (drop) until the official drop date. Following that date, students must be assigned a letter grade and can no longer drop the class. Students considering dropping the class should be aware of the 3-peat rule and the 6-drop rule so they can recognize how dropped classes may affect their academic success. The 6-drop rule refers to Texas law that dictates undergraduate students may not drop more than six courses during their undergraduate career. Courses dropped at other Texas public higher education institutions will count toward the six-course drop limit. The 3-peat rule refers to additional fees charged to students who take the same class for the third time.
